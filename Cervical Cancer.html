What is Cervical Cancer?

Cervical cancer is a malignant tumour that starts in the cervix, the lower part of the uterus. Nearly all cases stem from persistent infection with high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV) types.

Why it matters: Precancerous changes can exist for years without symptoms. Routine screening and HPV vaccination make cervical cancer one of the most preventable cancers worldwide.

Possible warning signs include abnormal vaginal bleeding (especially after intercourse), pelvic pain, or unusual discharge. Any symptoms should be evaluated by a qualified clinician.

Who May Be at Risk?

  • HPV infection (16 & 18): accounts for ~70 % of cases.
  • Early sexual activity or multiple partners.
  • Tobacco use: chemicals weaken cervical cells.
  • Weakened immunity: e.g., HIV infection.
  • Prolonged oral-contraceptive use (≥ 5 yr).
  • High parity: three or more full-term pregnancies.
  • Family history of cervical or other reproductive cancers.
  • Missed screening: no Pap/HPV tests for ≥ 5 yr.

Prevention & Treatment

Prevention

  • HPV vaccine: protects against the most oncogenic strains, ideally before sexual debut; licensed up to age 45.
  • Routine screening: Pap every 3 yr or HPV test every 5 yr (follow local guidelines).
  • Safer sex: condom use and limiting partners reduce HPV spread.
  • Quit smoking and maintain immune health.

Treatment

  • Early lesions: excision (LEEP, cone biopsy) or simple hysterectomy.
  • Invasive cancer: radical surgery and/or chemoradiation.
  • Advanced disease: chemotherapy, targeted agents, immunotherapy, plus supportive care.

All treatment decisions belong to a licensed healthcare team; early detection dramatically improves outcomes.
